Postherpetic Neuralgia

If you've been told
there's nothing
more to be done —

Postherpetic neuralgia (PHN) is the chronic nerve pain that follows a shingles infection. The rash heals. The pain does not. For many patients it persists for months, years, or decades — and most are told it's simply something they have to live with.

Dr. Aguila disagrees with that conclusion.

Burning, stabbing, or electric pain along the face, scalp, or body
Pain that persists long after the shingles rash has healed
Sensitivity to touch, clothing, or temperature changes
Failed gabapentin, pregabalin, nerve blocks, or other treatments
Told by other physicians that nothing more can be done

Am I a Candidate?

You may be a candidate if:

Surgery for PHN is not right for everyone — but for patients who meet certain criteria and have exhausted other options, it can offer relief that medications alone cannot provide.

01
You have a confirmed history of shingles with persistent nerve pain afterward
02
You have tried standard medications — gabapentin, pregabalin, amitriptyline — without lasting relief
03
Your pain has persisted for six months or longer after the shingles rash resolved
04
You are otherwise in good health for an elective surgical procedure

About Dr. Aguila

A surgeon who

Dr. Demetrio Aguila is a pain-focused peripheral nerve surgeon and the founder of Total Pain Solutions in Omaha, Nebraska. He is one of a small number of surgeons in the United States who specializes in surgical treatment for chronic pain — conditions that most patients have been told cannot be resolved.

Dr. Aguila trained under Dr. A. Lee Dellon, the physician who pioneered the field of peripheral nerve surgery for chronic pain. He is a diplomate of the American Board of Plastic Surgery and the American Board of Otolaryngology, a Fellow of the American College of Surgeons (FACS), and a member of the American Society of Peripheral Nerve (ASPN).

Patients travel from all 50 states and 32 countries to his practice in Omaha — not because it is close, but because the surgical treatment he provides is available from very few physicians anywhere in the world. His primary subspecialty focus is postherpetic neuralgia (PHN) — the chronic nerve pain that persists after shingles, most commonly affecting the face, scalp, and neck.

M25 Program

Pay for care with
community service.

The M25 Program is one of the most innovative approaches to healthcare access in the country. It gives patients who cannot afford surgery a way to pay by donating their time to community service — lifting the body, mind, and soul of patients and the community simultaneously.

The spiritual inspiration for the M25 Program comes from Matthew 25:40: "Whatever you did for one of these least brothers of mine, you did for me."

One of the key components in surgical success is postoperative compliance. Patients who are invested in their outcomes get better results — and the M25 Program creates that investment in a way that gives back to the community at the same time.

Total Pain Solutions is a direct care practice. We cut costs by eliminating insurance company middlemen and pass those savings directly to patients: no prior authorizations, no denials, no surprise bills. Patients can pay with cash, credit card, debit card, or check, or use a third party such as Care Credit or Proceed Finance. We are also happy to provide a superbill for patients who wish to submit to their own insurance for potential reimbursement.

Common questions
from patients like you.

Dr. Aguila believes informed patients get better results. These are the questions he hears most often.

Ask Your Own Question
Dr. Aguila specializes in treating patients with chronic pain due to various types of injuries to peripheral nerves. If your pain is due to injury or entrapment of one or more peripheral nerves, there is a very high chance he can help.
Nerve pain often presents as burning, electric, or shooting sensations. Common signs include numbness, tingling, pins and needles, or decreased sensation. Pain medication is often very ineffective at treating pain of nerve origin. The sensations can be so unusual that patients have difficulty describing them in words.
When the impact on your quality of life outweighs the risks of surgery. For patients whose quality of life is significantly compromised, the already low risks of peripheral nerve surgery fade to insignificance. Generally speaking, if you are otherwise healthy enough to tolerate a general anesthetic, the risks are very low.
For patients with peripheral neuropathy who qualify for nerve decompression surgery, very good or complete relief occurs about 80% of the time. When pain comes from a single damaged nerve where disconnecting the nerve is the best course, relief is achieved more than 99% of the time. For most peripheral nerve problems, the success rate is 80% or better.
It depends on how the nerve has been injured. If compressed, the solution typically involves removing tissue to open the space around the nerve. If the nerve is stuck in scar tissue, it may need to be freed so it can glide again. If cut, it may be sewn back together or repaired with a nerve graft. If damaged beyond repair, the nerve may be disconnected so it can no longer send a pain signal to the brain.
EMG and Nerve Conduction Velocity studies are rarely useful for diagnosing peripheral nerve problems. In complex cases they will usually be normal or identify only a small portion of the problem. The best way to diagnose a peripheral nerve problem is by taking a careful history combined with a comprehensive peripheral nerve examination.
Most physicians have not received formal training in the anatomy and physiology of the peripheral nervous system. Without this training, they haven't been equipped to identify a peripheral nerve injury when it presents itself. Additionally, most doctors are not aware that surgery is an option — Dr. Aguila's application of peripheral nerve surgery to treat pain is relatively new to the field.
